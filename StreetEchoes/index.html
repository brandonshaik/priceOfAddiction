Street Echoes of the Drug Epidemic

Zameena Mejia, Gustavo Martinez Contreras, and Jeremy Silk Smith


The streets of New York City are the places where the drug epidemics have been heard the loudest, for good and bad. Places like Charlotte Street in the South Bronx and the “Crack is Wack” playground in Harlem stand as testaments of the history of the crack epidemic.


Charlotte Street was emblematic of the urban decay that devastated The Bronx in the 60s and 70s. The couple of blocks that are Charlotte Street, had piles of rumble. The buildings that were standing were burned out. Presidents Carter, Reagan, and Clinton visited Charlotte Street to try to show that they understood the crack epidemic and its impact on American cities.

​

While presidents came to The Bronx to communicate their commitment to ending the crack epidemic, the streets continued to be ravaged by drugs and some artists took to the walls to serve as clarions of the danger consuming New Yorkers.

In the 1980s, New York City was plagued by crime and the black and Latino communities were hooked on a new and cheap drug of choice: crack cocaine
A 1990 U.S. Senate report estimated that New York state had 20 percent of the 2.2 million crack addicts in the United States
Artist Keith Haring decided to paint an anti-crack mural because his studio assistant became an addict.
In his words: "I was appalled by what was happening in the country, but especially New York, and seeing the slow reaction of the government to respond."
And although he almost went to trial because of he painted the mural without permission, he eventually was acquitted and the wall became a symbol of the fight against the crack plague.

For as long as the New York City Chief Medical Examiner's office has recorded data on unintentional drug overdose involving opioids, neighborhoods in South Bronx have consistently made it to the top five NYC neighborhoods in overdose death rates. But Daliah Heller, a clinical professor at the Graduate School of Public Health and Health Policy said one must interpret these numbers carefully to take into account densely populated areas.

"We've had higher rates of overdose in New York City than the rest of the country for a long time, but now the country is catching up and NYC might finally be doing a bit better," Heller said.

Heller adds that it is important that people understand the role that other drugs play in contributing to overdose death.


"As we put out messages about opioids and the role they play in overdose deaths, we also should be messaging about the role that mixing drugs plays in overdose death. The truth is it's the mixing drugs that often times leads to the actual overdose."

—Daliah Heller
